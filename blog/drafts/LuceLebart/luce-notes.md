# Notes on Luce Lebart — Photography Historian

## Who she is

Luce Lebart (born 1970, Asnières-sur-Seine) is a Paris/London-based photography historian, curator, and author.
She is French correspondent for the **Archive of Modern Conflict** (London) collection, one of the world's largest
private photography archives.

**Institutional roles:**
- Director, **Canadian Photography Institute** (CPI), Ottawa, 2016–2018
  - The CPI was founded to fill the gap left by the abrupt closure of the Canadian Museum of Contemporary Photography in 2009, backed by Scotiabank, the AMC, and the National Gallery of Canada Foundation.
  - The AMC donated the legendary **Matthew Isenburg Collection** (~10,000 items: daguerreotypes, stereoviews, 19th-c. cameras, books, manuscripts) to the National Gallery as part of an "Origins of Photography" collection.
- Collection director and curator, **Société française de photographie**, 2011–2016

**Research focus:** Archival photography, history of photographic techniques, scientific and documentary image practices.
Her key interest: photographs made *without artistic intention* that nonetheless carry enormous aesthetic potential.
She works to recover and promote forgotten or overlooked collections.

## Books (images on hand)

### 1. *Une histoire mondiale des femmes photographes* (2020, Textuel)
**English:** *A World History of Women Photographers* (Thames & Hudson, 2022)
Co-edited with **Marie Robert** (curator, Musée d'Orsay).

- Images: `FemmesPhotographes.jpg` (French, teal cover — woman in gold dress pointing gun)
  and `WomenPhotographers.jpg` (English, turquoise cover — close-up portrait)
- 300 women photographers, from the invention of the medium to the early 21st century
- 450 illustrations; 160 contributors from around the world, all women, to avoid a Western-centric view
- Research supported by Rencontres d'Arles and **Women In Motion** (Kering program)
- Published first in French (Nov 2020); English edition Thames & Hudson 2022

This is a landmark work — a deliberate act of historical correction. The structure (160 women writers
writing about 300 women photographers) is itself a methodological statement.

### 2. *Les Grands Photographes du XXe siècle* (Larousse, 2017)
- Image: `LesGrandesPhotographes.jpg` (Dorothea Lange's "Migrant Mother" on cover)
- A survey of the great photographers of the 20th century
- Solo-authored by Lebart; published by Larousse (mass-market French reference publisher)
- Interesting contrast with the women photographers book: this is the "canonical" history,
  the other is the corrective

### 3. *Premières Fois, Premières Photos* (Spector Books)
- Image: `IMG_5190.jpeg` — striking silver/grey cloth cover with botanical fern, rotated title
- Title translates as "Firsts, First Photos" — about photography's firsts/origins
- Publisher: Spector Books (Leipzig) — known for experimental/archival photobooks
- Fits her broader interest in the origins and early history of the medium

### 4. *Gold and Silver / Or et Argent* (RVB Books / Canadian Photography Institute, 2018)
- Image: `IMG_5198.jpeg` — title page photographed lying on bed
- Full title: *Gold and Silver: Daguerreotypes, Ambrotypes and Tintypes from the Gold Rush*
- Bilingual (EN/FR); published during her CPI directorship
- Explores early photographic processes (daguerreotype, ambrotype, tintype) through the specific
  historical context of the Gold Rush — photography and material culture intersecting
- Shown at MBAM (Montreal Museum of Fine Arts) and FOAM Amsterdam (2018)

### 5. *Inventions 1915–1938: From the Gas Mask to the Washing Machine* (RVB Books / CNRS)
- Image: `IMG_5199.jpeg` — cream/beige cover, minimalist typography
- Bilingual (EN/FR); CNRS co-publisher suggests a research/academic dimension
- Examines patent photographs and technical imagery from the interwar period
- This is quintessential Lebart: functional images (patent filings, product photography)
  treated as historical and aesthetic objects
- The title's span — gas mask to washing machine — captures the violent/domestic range of
  interwar invention perfectly

## Key themes and angles

**1. The aesthetics of the non-artistic image**
Her central intellectual preoccupation: photographs made for forensic, scientific, commercial, or administrative purposes
that exceed their functional intent. Patent photos, mold documentation, war inventions — all treated as objects
of historical and visual interest.

**2. Recovering the forgotten**
Consistently drawn to overlooked collections, neglected archives, and erased histories. The women photographers
book is the most visible example, but it runs through everything — forgotten techniques (non-silver processes),
forgotten photographers (Léon Gimpel), forgotten archives.

**3. Photography and materiality**
Strong interest in the material history of the medium: daguerreotypes, ambrotypes, tintypes, non-silver
processes. Not just images but objects with physical properties.

**4. Photography at the boundary of science and art**
Cloud classification, glaciology, forensic photography, patent imagery — areas where the camera was
deployed as a scientific instrument and produced images that are also, incidentally, beautiful.

**5. Gender and canon-formation**
The *World History* book is explicitly a challenge to the received canon. With 160 women writers
and 300 women subjects, it enacts its argument structurally, not just thematically.

## Personal connection (background, not the subject)

Luce Lebart is the daughter of **Ludovic Lebart** (b. 1942), French statistician, senior researcher at CNRS
and professor at the École Nationale Supérieure des Télécommunications, Paris. Ludovic is a first-degree
disciple of Jean-Paul Benzécri and a central figure in the French school of data analysis (*analyse des données*),
particularly correspondence analysis and textual data analysis. Key books: *Multivariate Descriptive Statistical
Analysis* (Wiley, 1984, with Morineau & Warwick), *Exploring Textual Data* (1998).

Ludovic Lebart is a fellow Chevalier — the personal connection to Luce's work runs through him.
This is worth a sentence in the post ("I came to her work through her father...") but the subject
is Luce and the recovery theme, not the family.

Note also the quiet symmetry: both Lebarts are, in their respective domains, rescuers of overlooked
traditions — he of the French statistical school that was long eclipsed by Anglo-American methods,
she of the women who made photography's history but were written out of it.

## Main angle: The recovery project

The central theme linking Luce Lebart's work across photography and Michael Friendly's work
in data visualization history is **the act of recovery** — the systematic effort to find and
restore contributions that were made, were significant, but were not absorbed into the canonical
narrative of a field.

**In photography (Lebart):**
- 300 women photographers, from the 1840s to the 2000s, working across every continent and genre
- Not marginal figures doing marginal work — many were central to the development of the medium,
  but overlooked because the history was written by and about men
- The canon is not a neutral record; it is a set of choices, and those choices have consequences

**In data visualization / statistics (Friendly):**
- Guerry: launched modern social science with thematic maps of crime in France (1833), then largely
  forgotten for 150 years
- Playfair: invented the bar chart, line graph, and pie chart — known, but underappreciated as a theorist
- Nightingale: her rose diagram saved lives, then she became a nurse who happened to make charts
- The French school of data analysis (correspondence analysis, Benzécri, Ludovic Lebart):
  eclipsed for decades by Anglo-American multivariate methods, now being rediscovered
- Many others in the *Milestones* project

**The shared structure:**
1. A field has a received canon — a shortlist of names, works, and "firsts"
2. The canon was assembled by people with particular blind spots (gender, language, nationality)
3. Scholars do recovery work: archival research, exhibition, republication, translation
4. The recovered figures are not curiosities — they change how we understand the field's development
5. The recovery is never complete; the archive is always larger than the canon

**What makes this interesting as a blog post:**
- It's personal (Ludovic → Luce, connection between two fields I care about)
- It generalizes: this is a structural feature of how disciplines form and reform themselves
- It has a data visualization dimension: how do you visualize what's been left out?
- Luce's books are beautiful objects — the post can be partly about what it feels like to hold them

**Possible opening:** I came across Luce Lebart's work through her father, Ludovic, whom I know as
a fellow Chevalier and as one of the great figures of French statistical analysis. When I learned what
his daughter had built a career doing, I recognized something familiar — the same impulse that has
driven my own work on the history of data visualization: the conviction that the received story is
incomplete, that the archive is larger than the canon, and that the missing figures matter.

festival. Recent themed exhibitions she curated there:
- *Avere vent'anni / Being Twenty* (2024)
- *Sky Album* (2024)
- *Europe Matters / Visions of a restless identity* (2023)

This is a significant ongoing platform — she's not just a historian/author but a major
festival director shaping contemporary photography discourse.
