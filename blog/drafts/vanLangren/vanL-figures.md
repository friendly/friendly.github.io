## Research notes on the 2011 Huylebrouck & Pauwels Eos article (for later)

Could not find the 2011 Eos piece itself online — it appears to be print-only
and was never digitized. Checked and came up empty: Huylebrouck's personal
site (dirkhuylebrouck.be), the current EOS Wetenschap site, KU Leuven's Lirias
repository, and ResearchGate.

What I did find instead:

- Huylebrouck wrote about the same material in English, in his "PI Guy" column
  on aNewDomain.net, crediting Jos Pauwels: ["On Michael Van Langren, The Moon
  and The Longitude
  Problem"](https://anewdomain.net/pi-guy-2-mysterious-code-is-the-key-to-the-longitude-problem/).
  Does *not* reproduce the cipher itself — just a moon-crater illumination
  photo credited to Pauwels. Does mention that "specialists think Van Langren
  used a so-called 'double Vigenère code'" — a guess later proven wrong by Van
  Eycke's actual solution, possibly worth a line as another "reasonable guess,
  also wrong."
- A 2020 academic paper, ["European Longitude Prizes I: Longitude
  Determination in the Spanish Empire"](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2009.12778)
  (arXiv 2009.12778), has a full Section 7 on van Langren (written before the
  cipher was solved — still calls it "as-yet-unsolved"). Useful for
  court-politics background. Includes a high-quality public-domain scan of
  the 1645 moon map (Figure 16, credited to Bibliothèque nationale de
  France) — possibly a better-sourced alternative to whatever moon-map image
  was already in hand.

Bottom line: the VRT image (#1 above) is still the only real source found for
the cipher/coded-manuscript image itself — nothing found beats it. Now
superseded anyway by the actual cipher image obtained directly (see update
above).
