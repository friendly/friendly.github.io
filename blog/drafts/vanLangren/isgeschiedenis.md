400-Year-Old Code of Michael van Langren Deciphered Within a Week

It sounds like a thriller: a scientist makes an extraordinary discovery but writes it in code, so it stays hidden for centuries. That's exactly what happened with a discovery by the 17th-century scientist Michael van Langren. Only four centuries later did two Belgian researchers manage to crack his code — and it took them just one week.

Background: Van Langren (b. 1598, Amsterdam; d. 1675, Brussels) was a cartographer, astronomer, and engineer who served as royal cosmographer to Philip IV of Spain. He created the first detailed map of the moon, and — thinking lunar mountain-peak illumination could serve as a universal clock visible simultaneously worldwide — tried to use it to solve the era's great navigational problem: determining longitude at sea. Latitude was measurable via instruments like the cross-staff, but longitude had no reliable method. He developed five approaches to the problem, two workable at sea, and pitched them to the Spanish court in Madrid in 1631 — where he got tangled in court intrigue and was rejected twice. One method he wrote down in 1634 and published in his 1645 book La verdadera longitud — but in an indecipherable code.

Why the code? Theories include fear of plagiarism, or that he himself doubted the method worked and wanted a hedge.

The crack: Amateur astronomer Jos Pauwels and KU Leuven mathematician Dirk Huylebrouck had long been fascinated by van Langren's work. After years of failed attempts by cryptographers worldwide, they reached Jarl Van Eycke — who, with David Oranchak and Sam Blake, had earlier taken six years to crack the Zodiac Killer cipher. This time it took him just one week. He found the spaces between words were meaningless and capitalization largely irrelevant; substituting letters for the digits revealed the text was written in 17th-century French, not Spanish as long assumed. Once Pauwels converted the archaic French to modern equivalents, the text was fully translated.

The method: Two identical cylindrical copper vessels with conical, pierced bottoms, filled with seawater, timed by the stars to drain empty in a known duration — essentially a water-clock (clepsydra) longitude method. You'd start the first vessel draining at departure while noting which known star sat on the meridian; when that star returned to the same position, the vessel should be empty. Sailors would repeat this daily against the stars to track longitude traveled.

Verdict: It didn't actually solve the longitude problem (that waited until 1730), and it wasn't especially groundbreaking even for its time — but it wasn't nonsense either, vindicating van Langren against the dismissal he faced at the Spanish court. A lunar crater, the Langrenus crater, honors him.
