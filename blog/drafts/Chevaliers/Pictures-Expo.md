# Pictures at an Exposition

*The second dispatch from Les Chevaliers des Albums de Statistique Graphique*

---

Paris, July 1900. The Exposition Universelle sprawled across the banks of
the Seine — seventy thousand exhibitors, fifty million visitors, the Eiffel
Tower presiding over it all with the satisfaction of something that had
survived its critics. The twentieth century was arriving, and the world had
come to Paris to watch.

Among the exhibitors, in the Palace of Social Economy, a young American had
arranged sixty-three charts on a wall.

He was thirty-two years old. His name was William Edward Burghardt Du Bois,
and he had spent the previous two years in Atlanta, working with his students
to produce something that had no precise precedent: a visual portrait of an
entire people, rendered in data, drawn by hand, presented to the world in
colours so vivid and forms so inventive that no one who saw them would
easily forget.

He called them *The Georgia Negro: A Social Study.*

---

## The Charts

They were unlike anything else at the Exposition.

The European tradition of statistical graphics — Playfair's bars and lines,
Minard's flows, Guerry's shaded maps, Cheysson's careful atlases — had been
largely in the service of states: trade flows, mortality rates, the
movements of armies. Data as the language of administration. Numbers as
instruments of governance.

Du Bois had taken the same tools and turned them toward something different.

His charts showed the population of Black Americans growing despite
everything history had thrown at it. They showed land ownership increasing,
painstakingly, year by year. They showed literacy rising in a single
generation from near zero to more than half. They showed occupations,
family structures, church membership, income — the full texture of a people
building a life under conditions that were designed to prevent exactly that.

And they showed the other side too. Mortality rates. Incarceration. The
weight of what had been done and was still being done.

The forms were radical. Spirals instead of bars. Irregular shapes that
seemed to pulse with feeling. Colours — deep red, gold, black, green —
that no government statistical office would have sanctioned. They were
scientific documents and they were, unmistakeably, works of art. The jury
awarded them a gold medal, which was the least they could do.

---

## The Visitors

Cheysson came on a Tuesday, which was his habit.

He was sixty-three, silver-haired, still carrying the authority of a man
who had shaped French official statistics for two decades. He stood before
the charts for a long time without speaking, which those who knew him would
have recognised as the highest form of attention he could pay.

The others arrived too, the way certain afternoons gather more figures
than the guest list strictly allows. Or seemed to. The light in the Palace
of Social Economy was particular that afternoon: the kind of light that
arrives at an angle and makes the edges of things less distinct than
usual. Cheysson would later say, to no one in particular, that he had felt
accompanied.

Minard, had he been there, would have recognised immediately what Du Bois
had done with the flow form — taken it from the rivers and roads and armies
and pressed it into the service of human time, human loss, human survival.
He would have approved. He had always believed that the best maps were
the ones that made you feel the weight of what they were measuring.

Guerry, had he been there, would have recognised the shaded maps of Georgia
counties — the darkness deepening where poverty concentrated, where
incarceration rates rose — as cousins of his own moral statistics maps of
France. He had spent his career asking whether crime and poverty followed
geography. Du Bois was asking the same question, with the same tools, and
arriving at answers Guerry had never had cause to face: that the geography
of suffering, in America, followed the geography of race with a precision
that left no room for innocent interpretation.

Nightingale, had she been there, would have gone straight to the mortality
charts. She would have seen in them the same argument she had made forty
years earlier with her rose diagrams: that death, when you mapped it
carefully enough, revealed its causes. That the causes were not natural.
That they could be changed.

---

## The Meeting

Du Bois was standing near his charts when Cheysson approached. The
conversation, as best I can reconstruct it, began with the gold medal —
Cheysson offered congratulations, Du Bois received them with the
particular courtesy of a man who had learned early that white approval
was a complicated gift.

Then Cheysson asked about the spiral forms. He had not seen anything
like them. Where had they come from?

Du Bois considered this. From the data, he said. The data had a shape,
and the shape was not always a bar or a line. Sometimes the shape was a
spiral — something that returned to the same point and found it changed.
Something that showed time not as a straight march forward but as a
recurring encounter with the same conditions, which either improved or
did not.

Cheysson was quiet for a moment.

*C'est juste*, he said. That is correct.

They stood together before the charts. Around them, the Exposition
continued its enormous business. Fifty million people were coming to
see the future. Two of them were standing in a gallery in the Palace of
Social Economy, looking at sixty-three hand-drawn charts, understanding
something about what data could do when it was made to serve justice
rather than merely administration.

---

## What the Charts Said

The charts said: we are here.

They said: we were brought here against our will, and everything since
has been arranged to limit what we could become, and we have become
something anyway.

They said: here is the evidence. Here are the numbers. Here is the shape
of a century, drawn by hand, in colour, for anyone who cares to look.

Du Bois understood — as Playfair had understood, as Minard had understood,
as Nightingale had understood — that a graphic is an argument. That the
choice of form is a choice of rhetoric. That numbers do not speak for
themselves; they speak through the shapes we give them, and the shapes
we give them reflect what we believe is worth seeing.

He had made sixty-three arguments. The jury had given him a gold medal.
History had largely forgotten the charts for most of the following century,
until scholars and designers, in our own time, rediscovered them and
understood what they were looking at.

We, the Chevaliers, count ourselves among the grateful.

---

## Coda: Atlanta, Later

Du Bois went back to Atlanta. He went on to write *The Souls of Black Folk*,
to found the NAACP, to edit *The Crisis* for twenty-four years, to live
until 1963 — long enough to see the March on Washington, though he did not
see the Civil Rights Act. He died in Ghana, aged ninety-five, the night
before the march.

The charts went into storage somewhere and were not seen again for decades.

But they had been seen. Cheysson had seen them. The jury had seen them.
Fifty million people had come to Paris that summer, and some of them had
walked through the Palace of Social Economy and stopped, and looked, and
understood something they had not understood before.

That is what graphics are for.

---

*The Georgia Negro charts are now held at the Library of Congress. Facsimile
editions have been published, and they are as startling now as they were in
1900. If you have not seen them, see them.*

*W.E.B. Du Bois, 1868–1963.*
