# Les Chevaliers at the Café de Lilas

*The first in a new series: dispatches from Les Chevaliers des Albums de Statistique Graphique*

---

I turned 81 this past May. I am proud of my Milestones Project — the long
work of tracing the history of data visualization through the people and
moments that shaped it — but there are still stories to tell, and this is
one of them.

This is the story of an afternoon in Paris, a forgotten tomb, and a menu
that should not have existed.

---

## I. The Tomb

It was Antoine de Falguerolles who found it.

We had been wandering the older sections of Montparnasse cemetery for the
better part of an hour — Giles Palsky, Michael Greenacre, Antoine, and
myself — moving through Section 7 with a GPS coordinate and diminishing
confidence. The afternoon light was low and golden, the way Parisian autumn
light always seems to arrange itself for maximum atmosphere, and the stone
we were looking for had not announced itself.

Then Antoine stopped and crouched down.

The inscription was barely legible. Time and weather had done their patient
work on the stone, softening the letters toward illegibility, as if the
nineteenth century were slowly reclaiming what remained of its own. We
stood around it and read what we could.

*Charles Joseph Minard. 1781–1870.*

We had come prepared. The plaque we had made — a reproduction of the great
flow map of Napoleon's Russian campaign, rendered in ggplot2 with rather
more care than the original occasion perhaps demanded — fitted precisely
against the stone. Below the map, in clean lettering:

> *Ici repose Charles Joseph Minard*  
> *Ingénieur des Ponts et Chaussées*  
> *Auteur de la Carte Figurative des pertes successives en hommes*  
> *de l'Armée française dans la campagne de Russie, 1812–1813*

We fixed it in place. Someone took a photograph. Nobody said anything for
a while, which felt appropriate.

It was Giles who noticed the stone in Section 9, half-hidden by ivy, a
short walk away.

*André Michel Guerry. 1802–1866.*

Together in death as they had been in intellectual life: two men who had
each understood, independently and in their different ways, that numbers
could be made to speak if you gave them the right visual form. Neither had
known, presumably, when they chose their plots, that the other would be
nearby. Or perhaps the arrondissement had its own logic, drawing toward
it those who had tried to make the invisible visible.

We went to the Café de Lilas to celebrate.

---

## II. The Café

The Café de Lilas on Boulevard du Montparnasse has been many things to many
people. Picasso drank here. Hemingway wrote here, or said he did. Simone
de Beauvoir held court at a corner table for so many years that the table
acquired a kind of biographical gravity. The café has absorbed a great deal
of history with the equanimity of an institution that knows it will outlast
everyone who passes through.

We took a table near the window and ordered wine.

Les Chevaliers des Albums de Statistique Graphique — our fellowship, named
in honour of the extraordinary statistical atlases produced between 1879
and 1899 under the direction of Émile Cheysson for the French government —
had been meeting irregularly for years, in various cities, in various states
of scholarly enthusiasm. But this felt like a proper occasion. We had found
the tomb. We had fixed the plaque. We had found *both* tombs. Someone
ordered a second bottle.

It was then that Giles noticed the menu.

---

## III. The Menu

It had slipped down behind the wooden frame of the banquette, lodged in
the gap between cushion and wall with the patience of something that had
been waiting a long time to be found. The paper was heavy, cream-coloured,
slightly foxed at the edges. The date printed at the top read, in an
elegant serif typeface: *15 octobre 1879.*

The menu itself was unremarkable — the usual elaborate progression of a
serious Parisian lunch, from potage through entrée to dessert, with wines
noted in a careful hand. What was not unremarkable were the margins.

Someone had been drawing.

Along the left edge, in a confident ink faded to the colour of strong tea,
ran a series of flow lines — thick and thin, braided and diverging — that
any of us would have recognised anywhere. Below it, in a different hand,
a small rose diagram: petals of unequal size, radiating from a centre
point, with tiny annotations in a script none of us could immediately read.

On the back, squeezed between the *fromage* and the *café*, was a map of
France divided into departments, with shading that darkened toward the north
— the distribution of something, though what was not labelled. Next to it,
a neat column of figures, and the word *mortalité* underlined twice.

There were four sets of handwriting. Possibly five.

We called the waiter.

---

## IV. Marcel

His name was Marcel, and he moved through the café with the unhurried
authority of a man who has never been in a hurry and does not intend to
start. He was, at a conservative estimate, somewhere in his mid-eighties,
though he carried his age the way the café carried its history — as a
resource rather than a burden.

We showed him the menu.

He looked at it for a long time without speaking. Then he said, in French,
that his grandfather had told him about the old group. Academics, his
grandfather had called them, though that was not quite the right word —
men who cared about numbers and maps and the representation of things that
were difficult to see. They had come on Tuesday lunchtimes, always the same
table, always a long lunch. His grandfather had served them.

His grandfather had a name for them, too, written in the margin of the
reservations book where a table needed a label: *L'Équipe Graphique.* Not
a name the group had chosen for itself, Marcel thought — more likely
something his grandfather had needed for his own bookkeeping — but it had
survived three generations of family memory, which is its own kind of
christening, and it seemed to all of us exactly right.

We asked who they were.

Marcel considered this. Cheysson, he said, was always the host — a large
man, energetic, who talked with his hands. There was an older one who said
little but drew constantly on whatever paper was available: menus, napkins,
the backs of envelopes. And a woman, later, who arrived from England and
argued with all of them about everything and was always right. His
grandfather had liked her best.

He handed the menu back with the care of someone returning something that
did not entirely belong to the present.

*Mon grand-père disait qu'ils changeaient le monde*, he said. *Mais
doucement. Avec des graphiques.*

> My grandfather said they were changing the world. Quietly. With graphics.

He went to attend to another table, unhurried, as if the conversation had
been entirely unremarkable.

I have since done some further research, and L'Équipe Graphique, it turns
out, did not confine its activities to a banquette on the Boulevard du
Montparnasse. The
Statistical Society of London — not yet Royal, that honour came in 1887 —
held its Jubilee meetings in 1885, fifty years on from its founding, and the
published proceedings record a survey paper by Cheysson tracing the
progress of graphical method from Playfair's bars and ribbons through
Minard's flow maps to the statistical atlases of his own ministry. W.
Stanley Jevons spoke at the same gathering, returning to the price diagrams
he had built decades earlier to track the value of the currency since 1782
— proof, if any were needed, that the English and the French had been
drawing the same conclusions, in different ink, from opposite sides of the
Channel. Étienne-Jules Marey is not listed among the speakers; he was in
the south that year, photographing the flight of gulls with his
chronophotographic gun, and could not easily have been spared. But he
belongs in the room regardless, the way certain people belong in a
photograph taken before they arrived, and L'Équipe Graphique — by all
accounts, including Marcel's — always raised a glass to him in absentia. A
custom, evidently, the Chevaliers have been pleased to inherit.

---

## V. Together in Death as in Life

We should note, for the record, that the historical facts are not quite
cooperative with our fantasy. Minard died in 1870; Guerry in 1866. Cheysson
was the custodian of their legacy more than their contemporary. William
Farr, the great English statistician, died in 1883 — so his presence at a
Tuesday lunch in 1879 was at least chronologically possible. Florence
Nightingale lived until 1910, and the image of her arriving from London to
argue with a table of French statisticians and being invariably right has
a ring of plausibility about it, whatever the calendar says.

They could not all have been here.

And yet here was the menu. Here were the drawings. Here was Marcel, whose
grandfather had served them, and whose grandfather's grandfather had perhaps
served someone else, in a chain of memory that the café had quietly
maintained without making any fuss about it.

We raised our glasses.

To Minard and Guerry, who lie a few hundred metres apart in Montparnasse
and are now, at last, properly marked. To Sandra Rendgen, whose magnificent
catalogue of Minard's work restored him to the prominence he deserved and
without which we might never have come looking. To Lee Wilkinson, whose
Grammar of Graphics showed us how to recreate that map in code — and gave
us the plaque to bring with us. To Cheysson, who understood that beautiful
data was a form of argument. To Farr and Nightingale, who understood that
a good graph could empty a hospital ward or fill a graveyard, depending on
which side of the argument you were on.

To the Albums. To the Chevaliers. To Tuesday lunches and margins full of
drawings and the long, slow work of making the invisible visible.

*Santé.*

---

## Postscript

The menu is currently in the possession of Giles Palsky, who has promised
to have it examined by someone who knows about such things. Antoine believes
the rose diagram is in Nightingale's hand. Michael Greenacre is unconvinced
but has not ruled it out.

Marcel, when we left, was serving a young couple at the table by the door.
He did not look up. He had other things to attend to, and a long memory is
not the same thing as a preoccupation.

We will go back next year.
