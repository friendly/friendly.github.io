## French has Feelings About Temperature


English collapses distinctions that French insists on keeping separate. *Frais* and *froid* are not the same thing: *frais* is pleasantly cool, the word you use for a wine served correctly or a morning breeze that makes you glad to be alive. *Froid* is just cold — no compliment intended.


The French vocabulary of thermal sensation encodes hedonic valence that English leaves to context. *Tiède* (lukewarm) carries faint disapproval — a *tiède* reception to your ideas is not a good sign. *Doux* (mild, gentle) flatters the weather. *Moite* captures that particular beach-air heaviness, warm and slightly damp, the atmosphere that clings. *Étouffant* (stifling) and *brûlant* (burning) escalate past *chaud* the way English "scorching" escalates past "hot" — but the French get there faster.


The sommelier's *un peu frais* is perhaps the most elegant specimen: "slightly cool," deployed as reassurance, implying that the wine knows what it's doing. It is understatement as aesthetic judgment.


Canadian English has its own thermal poetry, especially in the north and west: *skookum cold* (you _really_ better bundle up) among them — where the sheer physiological extremity of winter demands vocabulary that standard English never had occasion to evolve.
Winnipegers, who call their city "Winterpeg" don't even call it "cold" until the temperature plunges below -30C.


What emerges, cross-linguistically, is a picture consistent with the psychophysics: temperature language is not neutral description. It is evaluation all the way down.
